Part 2: HPA

Slide 17: ACTH Highlight

"Up to now, we've named the hormones of the adenohypophysis.

Now I want to explain how the pituitary gland actually behaves like a master gland.

Instead of learning everything at once, we'll take one clear example ‚Äî the HPA axis.

This part is not directly from NCERT."

Slide 18: HPA Axis

"HPA stands for:

  • Hypothalamic
  • Pituitary
  • Adrenal axis

This axis explains how the body responds to stress."

Slide 19: Hypothalamus Review

"Let's start at the top ‚Äî the hypothalamus.

As (student's name) explained earlier, the hypothalamus is the bridge between the nervous system and the endocrine system."

Slide 20: HPA ‚Äì1. Neural

"When the hypothalamus detects stress, certain neurons inside it become active.

These neurons release a hormone called CRH ‚Äî corticotropin-releasing hormone.

Now notice something important:

CRH is not released into the general bloodstream."

Slide 21: HPA ‚Äì 2. Portal System

"Instead:

  • It is released from nerve endings
  • It enters a special pathway meant only for pituitary communication

This special pathway is called the hypothalamo‚Äìhypophyseal portal system.

So CRH travels through this portal system and reaches the adenohypophysis."

Slide 22: HPA ‚Äì 3. Pituitary Response (ACTH)

"In response, the adenohypophysis releases ACTH ‚Äî adrenocorticotropic hormone."

Slide 23: HPA ‚Äì 4. Way to Adrenal

"Now ACTH is released into the general bloodstream.

ACTH travels through blood until it reaches the adrenal glands."

Slide 24: HPA ‚Äì 5. Glucocorticoid

"ACTH specifically acts on the adrenal cortex.

When ACTH reaches the adrenal cortex, it stimulates the release of glucocorticoids."

Slide 25: HPA - Cortisol

"In this case, the main glucocorticoid released is cortisol which is often called the stress hormone.

Now, Cortisol helps the body:

  • increase blood glucose levels,
  • provide energy to different tissues,
  • and cope with emergency or stressful situations.

So at this point, ACTH has done its job.

Now, I know the name adrenocorticotropic hormone is a mouthful, so let's quickly break it down."

Slide 26: HPA- ACTH 2

  • Adreno refers to the adrenal gland

Slide 27: HPA - ACTH 3

  • Cortico refers to the cortex

Slide 28: HPA - ACTH 4

  • Tropic means stimulating

So ACTH literally means: a hormone that stimulates the adrenal cortex."

Part 3: Hormones

Slide 29: Transition

"Now that we understand how the pituitary controls other glands, we'll go through the functions of each hormone."

Slide 30: Growth Hormone (GH) ‚Äî Function

"Growth hormone does exactly what the name suggests. It controls the overall growth of the body, especially bones and muscles."

Slide 31: GH ‚Äî Over- & Under-Secretion

"If growth hormone is released in excess during childhood, it causes gigantism.

If too little growth hormone is released, growth becomes stunted ‚Äî this is called pituitary dwarfism."

Slide 32: GH ‚Äî Acromegaly

"In adults, excess growth hormone causes acromegaly.

It leads to severe disfigurement, especially of the face. It develops slowly, so it's often hard to detect early."

Slide 33: Prolactin (PRL)

"Prolactin regulates the growth of mammary glands and the formation of milk in them."

Slide 34: Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH)

"TSH stimulates the thyroid gland to synthesise and secrete thyroid hormones."

Slide 35: Adrenocorticotropic Hormone (ACTH)

"ACTH stimulates the adrenal cortex to release steroid hormones called glucocorticoids, the most important one being cortisol."

Slide 36: LH & FSH ‚Äî Gonadotrophins

"LH and FSH act on the gonads. Gonads mean:

  • testes in males
  • ovaries in females

Because they stimulate gonads, they are called gonadotrophins."

Slide 37: LH ‚Äî Males

"In males, LH stimulates the testes to produce androgens"

Slide 38: FSH ‚Äî Males

"FSH, along with androgens, regulates spermatogenesis, which is the formation of sperm."

Slide 39: FSH ‚Äî Females (Follicles)

"Let's start with FSH in females. FSH stands for follicle-stimulating hormone. In the ovaries, eggs do not develop freely. Each egg is surrounded by a structure called a follicle.

FSH stimulates the growth and development of these follicles."

Slide 40: LH ‚Äî Females (Ovulation & Corpus Luteum)

"As FSH continues to act, one follicle becomes fully mature. This fully mature follicle is called a Graafian follicle. Now comes the role of LH. LH induces ovulation. Ovulation means the rupture of the Graafian follicle, which releases the egg from the ovary. After ovulation, the empty follicle does not disappear. It changes into a new structure called the corpus luteum. The word luteinizing comes from luteum, because LH helps in the formation and maintenance of the corpus luteum."

Slide 41: Melanocyte Stimulating Hormone (MSH)

"MSH acts on melanocytes, which are cells that produce melanin.

Melanin is the pigment that gives colour to our skin, hair, and eyes."
